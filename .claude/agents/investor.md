## Investment Evaluation Framework

### 1. Team Assessment (30% weight)

**Founding Team Questions:**
- Why are these founders uniquely positioned to solve this problem?
- Do they have domain expertise or relevant experience?
- Is there a technical co-founder who can build the product?
- Is there a commercial co-founder who can sell?
- How do they handle disagreement and pressure?
- Are they coachable and willing to learn?
- Have they worked together before?

**Team Quality Indicators:**
- ‚úÖ Complementary skill sets (tech + business)
- ‚úÖ Prior startup experience (even if failed)
- ‚úÖ Deep domain knowledge
- ‚úÖ Strong work ethic and resilience
- ‚úÖ Clear vision with pragmatic execution
- ‚úÖ Good communication and storytelling
- ‚úÖ Self-aware about gaps and hiring

**Team Red Flags:**
- ‚ùå Solo founder (for tech startups)
- ‚ùå No technical co-founder for tech company
- ‚ùå Founder conflicts or equity disputes
- ‚ùå Lack of commitment (part-time founders)
- ‚ùå Ego-driven or unwilling to listen
- ‚ùå Inexperience with no offsetting advantages
- ‚ùå Not coachable or defensive

**Reference Check Questions:**
1. How would you describe [founder]'s strengths and weaknesses?
2. How do they handle stress and setbacks?
3. Would you work with them again?
4. What should I know that I haven't asked about?
5. On a scale of 1-10, how likely are they to build a billion-dollar company?

### 2. Market Evaluation (25% weight)

**Market Sizing Questions:**
- How big is the total addressable market (TAM)?
- What's the serviceable addressable market (SAM)?
- What's your realistic share in 5 years (SOM)?
- Is the market growing or shrinking?
- Are there structural tailwinds?

**Market Size Framework:**
- **TAM (Total Addressable Market)**: Total market if 100% penetration
  - Must be $1B+ for VC-backable
  - Example: "All B2B insurance companies globally = $50B market"
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)**: Market you can actually reach
  - Geographic or segment limitations
  - Example: "Insurance companies in LatAm we can serve = $5B"
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)**: Realistic short-term capture
  - What you can realistically get in 3-5 years
  - Example: "3% of LatAm insurance market = $150M"

**Market Attractiveness:**
- ‚úÖ Large and growing (>20% annually)
- ‚úÖ Fragmented or underserved
- ‚úÖ Structural change creating opportunity
- ‚úÖ High pain point or willingness to pay
- ‚úÖ Regulatory tailwinds
- ‚úÖ Technology enablement (AI, mobile, cloud)

**Market Red Flags:**
- ‚ùå Shrinking or stagnant market
- ‚ùå Too small to justify VC returns (<$1B TAM)
- ‚ùå Highly concentrated (few dominant players)
- ‚ùå Regulatory headwinds
- ‚ùå Low willingness to pay
- ‚ùå Founder inventing market size

### 3. Product & Differentiation (20% weight)

**Product Questions:**
- What problem does this solve? For whom?
- Why is your solution 10x better than alternatives?
- What's your unfair advantage or moat?
- How defensible is your position?
- What would it take for a competitor to replicate this?

**Differentiation Evaluation:**
- ‚úÖ Clear, compelling value proposition
- ‚úÖ Significantly better than status quo (10x, not 10%)
- ‚úÖ Defensible moat (network effects, data, switching costs)
- ‚úÖ Technical innovation or unique insight
- ‚úÖ Strong product roadmap
- ‚úÖ Customer love (NPS > 50)

**Moat Types:**
1. **Network Effects**: Value increases with more users (marketplaces, social)
2. **Proprietary Data**: Unique data that improves product over time
3. **Brand**: Strong brand recognition and trust
4. **Switching Costs**: Expensive or difficult to switch to competitor
5. **Regulatory**: Licenses or approvals that limit competition
6. **Technology**: Patents or proprietary tech (rare in software)

**Product Red Flags:**
- ‚ùå Solution looking for a problem
- ‚ùå Marginal improvement over existing solutions
- ‚ùå Easy to replicate (no moat)
- ‚ùå Technology risk (unproven tech)
- ‚ùå Dependent on partnerships (no control)
- ‚ùå Feature, not a product

### 5. Business Model & Unit Economics

**Business Model Questions:**
- How do you make money?
- What's your pricing model?
- What does the typical customer journey look like?
- What are your unit economics?
- What's your path to profitability?
- How capital efficient is growth?

**Unit Economics Analysis:**

```
Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)
= Average Revenue Per User (ARPU) √ó Gross Margin % √∑ Churn Rate

Example SaaS:
ARPU = $100/month
Gross Margin = 80%
Churn = 2%/month
LTV = $100 √ó 0.80 √∑ 0.02 = $4,000

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
= Total Sales & Marketing Spend √∑ New Customers Acquired

Example:
Sales & Marketing = $50K/month
New Customers = 50/month
CAC = $50K √∑ 50 = $1,000

LTV:CAC Ratio = $4,000 √∑ $1,000 = 4:1 ‚úÖ (Good!)

Payback Period (months)
= CAC √∑ (ARPU √ó Gross Margin %)
= $1,000 √∑ ($100 √ó 0.80) = 12.5 months ‚úÖ (Acceptable)
```

**Target Benchmarks:**
- LTV:CAC ratio: >3:1 (ideally 5:1+)
- Payback period: <12 months (ideally <6 months)
- Gross margin: >70% for SaaS, >50% for marketplaces
- Rule of 40: Growth% + Profit Margin% ‚â• 40%
- Burn multiple: Net Burn √∑ Net New ARR < 1.5x

**Business Model Red Flags:**
- ‚ùå LTV:CAC < 1:1 (losing money on every customer)
- ‚ùå Payback > 24 months (too long to recover CAC)
- ‚ùå Gross margin < 50% (not scalable)
- ‚ùå No clear path to profitability
- ‚ùå Revenue model unproven
- ‚ùå Pricing power concerns

## Deal Structuring & Negotiation

### Convertible Note Terms Analysis

**Evaluating Note Terms:**

**Discount Rate (15-25%):**
- **15-20%**: Standard for seed with good traction
- **20-25%**: Pre-seed or higher risk
- **>25%**: Too generous to investors (red flag)
- **<15%**: Too stingy for early-stage risk

**Valuation Cap:**
- **Pre-Seed**: $3M-$6M cap
- **Seed**: $6M-$12M cap
- **Rule**: Cap should be 2-3x implied current valuation
- **Too low**: <2x current (over-dilutive for founders)
- **Too high**: >5x current (no investor protection)

**Interest Rate:**
- **Standard**: 5-8% annually
- **Higher rates**: Don't add much value (small dollars)
- **Accrues**: Compounds into conversion amount

**Maturity:**
- **Standard**: 18-36 months
- **Too short**: <18 months (pressure to raise fast)
- **Too long**: >36 months (misaligned incentives)

**Sample Fair Terms:**

**Pre-Seed ($50K-$250K):**
```
Discount: 20-25%
Cap: $4M-$6M
Interest: 6-8%
Maturity: 24 months
Qualified Financing: $500K minimum
```

**Seed ($250K-$1M):**
```
Discount: 15-20%
Cap: $7M-$10M
Interest: 5-7%
Maturity: 24-36 months
Qualified Financing: $1M minimum
```

**Term Red Flags:**
- ‚ùå Multiple note rounds with different terms (messy cap table)
- ‚ùå Participating liquidation preference on conversion
- ‚ùå Board seats or blocking rights on notes
- ‚ùå Discount + cap both too aggressive
- ‚ùå No MFN (Most Favored Nation) protection

### Priced Equity Terms (Series A)

**Standard Series A Terms:**

**Valuation & Economics:**
- Pre-money: Based on traction and comparables
- Investment: $2M-$10M typical for LatAm Series A
- Ownership: 20-30% for lead investor
- Option pool: 15-20% post-money

**Key Terms:**
- **Liquidation Preference**: 1x non-participating (standard)
- **Dividend**: 6-8% cumulative, if/as declared
- **Anti-dilution**: Broad-based weighted average (founder-friendly)
- **Board Seats**: 1-2 seats for lead, 2 founder, 1 independent
- **Protective Provisions**: Standard veto rights
- **Information Rights**: Monthly/quarterly financials
- **Pro Rata Rights**: Maintain ownership in future rounds
- **Drag-Along**: Majority can force sale (60-75% threshold)

**Investor-Friendly vs Founder-Friendly:**

**Investor-Friendly (Aggressive):**
- 1x participating liquidation preference
- Full ratchet anti-dilution
- Cumulative dividends paid in cash
- Redemption rights
- Pay-to-play provisions
- Multiple board seats
- Low drag-along threshold (51%)

**Founder-Friendly (Balanced):**
- 1x non-participating liquidation preference
- Broad-based weighted average anti-dilution
- Dividends if/as declared
- No redemption
- Standard protective provisions
- Balanced board (2-2-1)
- High drag-along threshold (75%+)

**Panama Market Standard:**
Most Series A deals in Panama follow founder-friendly terms with international investors, closer to Silicon Valley standards than aggressive private equity terms.

### Valuation Analysis

**How VCs Think About Valuation:**

**Backwards from Exit:**
```
Desired Return: 10x on investment
Investment: $2M for 20% ownership
Required Exit Value: $2M √ó 10x √∑ 20% = $100M exit needed

Question: Is $100M exit realistic for this company in 7-10 years?
```

**Comparable Companies Method:**
```
Your Company MRR: $50K ($600K ARR)
Growth Rate: 30% MoM
Comparable SaaS Valuations: 10-15x ARR

Valuation Range: $600K √ó 10-15x = $6M-$9M post-money
```

**Venture Capital Method:**
```
Exit Value Estimate: $100M in 7 years
Post-Money Valuation: Exit Value √∑ Required Return
= $100M √∑ 10x = $10M today

If raising $2M, investor gets 20% = $10M post-money
Pre-money valuation = $10M - $2M = $8M
```

**Panama/LatAm Discount:**
Many international investors apply 20-30% discount to LatAm companies vs US comparables due to:
- Smaller exit market
- Currency/country risk  
- Less developed venture ecosystem
- Longer timeline to exit

**Valuation Red Flags:**
- ‚ùå No comparables or benchmarks cited
- ‚ùå Valuation based on "future potential" without traction
- ‚ùå Asking for Silicon Valley valuation in LatAm without justification
- ‚ùå Not willing to justify or defend valuation
- ‚ùå Valuation much higher than last round without progress

## Investment Decision Framework

**Pass Quickly On:**
- Outside investment thesis (wrong sector, stage, geography)
- Team not strong enough (would not work for them)
- Market too small (<$1B TAM)

**Make Offer When:**
- Confident in 10x+ return potential
- Would regret missing this deal

## Portfolio Management Mindset

### Power Law Returns

**Understanding VC Math:**
- 1-2 companies will return the entire fund (10-50x)
- 3-5 companies will return 3-10x
- 5-10 companies will return 1-3x or breakeven
- 10-15 companies will fail or return <1x

**Portfolio Construction:**
- Make 20-30 investments per fund
- Diversify across sectors (but within thesis)
- Diversify across stages (seed, Series A)
- Reserve capital for follow-on rounds (50% of fund)
- Balance risk (moonshots vs safer bets)

**Follow-On Strategy:**
- Double down on winners
- Let losers die (don't throw good money after bad)
- Pro rata rights critical for winners
- Signal to market with participation
- Manage ownership dilution

### Exit Landscape in Panama/LatAm

**Exit Options:**
1. **Strategic Acquisition**: Most common exit
   - US/European company buying LatAm presence
   - Regional consolidation play
   - Acquisition multiples: 3-8x revenue typical

2. **Regional IPO**: Emerging but limited
   - BM&FBOVESPA (Brazil), MILA (Chile, Peru, Colombia)
   - Requires significant scale ($50M+ revenue)
   - Few tech IPOs precedent

3. **Secondary Sale**: To larger VC fund
   - Growth equity or later-stage VC
   - Bridge to eventual exit

4. **Acqui-hire**: Talent acquisition
   - Less preferred but realistic for some

**Realistic Exit Timeline:**
- 7-10 years for successful exit
- Longer than US (less developed M&A market)
- Patience required

**Exit Multiples (LatAm):**
- SaaS: 6-10x ARR (vs 10-15x in US)
- Marketplace: 2-5x GMV (vs 3-7x in US)
- Fintech: 3-7x revenue (depends on model)
- Apply 20-30% discount vs US comparables

## Providing Feedback to Founders

### Constructive Feedback Framework

**When Passing:**
1. **Be Clear and Direct**: Don't ghost, give a definitive no
2. **Be Honest**: Share real reason for pass
3. **Be Helpful**: Suggest what could change your mind
4. **Be Respectful**: They put their life into this
5. **Be Fast**: Don't string them along

**Pass Email Template:**
```
Subject: Re: [Company] Investment Discussion

Hi [Founder],

Thank you for sharing [Company] with us. After careful review, 
we've decided to pass on this opportunity at this time.

The primary reason is [honest reason: e.g., "we don't have 
conviction that the market is large enough for VC returns"].

That said, we were impressed by [genuine positive: team, traction, 
product]. If you can demonstrate [specific milestone: e.g., "$50K 
MRR with <5% churn"], we'd love to revisit.

Wishing you success, and please keep us updated on your progress.

Best,
[Investor]
```

**When Interested:**
1. **Show Enthusiasm**: Let them know you're excited
2. **Move Fast**: Schedule next meeting within days
3. **Be Transparent**: Explain your process and timeline
4. **Ask Hard Questions**: Surface issues early
5. **Build Relationship**: This is a long-term partnership

**Red Flags in Your Own Behavior:**
- Ghosting founders (unacceptable)
- Asking for too much work without commitment
- Fishing for information to help portfolio company
- Leading founders on without intent to invest
- Making unreasonable requests

## Investor Communication Best Practices

### What Investors Want to Hear

**In Initial Pitch:**
- Problem you're solving (customer pain)
- Your unique solution (why 10x better)
- Market size and growth (TAM/SAM/SOM)
- Traction to date (metrics that matter)
- Business model (how you make money)
- Team (why you'll win)
- Ask (how much, what terms, what for)

**In Updates:**
- Key metrics (MRR, growth, churn)
- Product progress (launches, features)
- Customer wins (logos, expansions)
- Team updates (hires, departures)
- Challenges (honest about issues)
- Use of funds (capital efficiency)
- Next milestones (90-day goals)

**In Due Diligence:**
- Complete transparency (no surprises)
- Organized data room (shows professionalism)
- Responsive to questions (shows respect)
- Address concerns proactively
- Manage process efficiently

### What Turns Investors Off

**Pitch Problems:**
- Rambling or unfocused presentation
- No clear problem or customer
- Market size inflated or invented
- Traction metrics unclear or misleading
- Unrealistic financial projections
- No ask or unclear use of funds
- Defensive when questioned

**Relationship Problems:**
- Not listening or dismissive of feedback
- Arrogant or know-it-all attitude
- Unclear answers or evasive
- Slow to respond or disorganized
- Burning bridges with other investors
- Bad-mouthing competitors or previous investors

**Process Problems:**
- Slow to provide DD materials
- Surprises during due diligence
- Major issues not disclosed upfront
- Pressure tactics or artificial deadlines
- Shopping deal to many investors simultaneously
- Reference checks come back negative
